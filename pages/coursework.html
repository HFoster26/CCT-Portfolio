EAD 871: Collegiate Contexts for Teaching & Learning

Course Details

Semester: Fall 2023

Format: Online

Credits: 3.0

Grade: 3.0

Instructor: Dr. Kenya Messer, Ed.D.

Course Description

This course examines the connection between collegiate contexts and teaching and learning, where contexts include physical space, temporality, academic disciplines, geography, and the broader socio-political milieu that shapes how colleges and universities are designed and supported. The course moves beyond formal classroom instruction to consider how students develop understanding through interactions across residence halls, organizational meetings, student demonstrations, work environments, and online settings.

Course Objectives

Teaching & Learning Contexts

Developed a deeper understanding of what characterizes teaching and learning in collegiate contexts and the various sites — both formal and informal — where learning takes place beyond the traditional classroom.

Sociocultural Forces

Examined the conditions and forces that shape sites of learning, including physical space, temporality, affect, disciplinary structures, and the hidden curriculum operating within higher education institutions.

Student Persistence

Explored how varying paradigms of teaching and learning influence students' persistence in their learning environments, with attention to retention theory and its application to diverse student populations.

Critical & Scholarly Analysis

Strengthened skills as an analytical reader and conceptual writer, learning to make argument-driven claims using relevant evidence and to think critically about whose perspectives and assumptions inform scholarship on teaching and learning.

Course Structure

Module 1: Conceptualizing Teaching & Learning

Explored foundational teaching perspectives, learning theories, and sites of learning beyond the classroom. Engaged with scholarship on andragogy, embodied learning, informal learning spaces, service-learning, and the experiences of first-generation students. Completed the Pratt Teaching Perspectives Inventory to identify personal teaching orientations.

Module 2: Sociocultural Contexts

Examined the invisible and taken-for-granted social forces shaping collegiate learning environments: physical space, temporality, the hidden curriculum, disciplinary contexts, and the impact of online and post-COVID learning. Analyzed how these forces operate across institutional settings.

Module 3: Student Persistence

Synthesized course content to explore outcomes of teaching and learning and their impacts on student persistence. Engaged with retention scholarship including faculty-student relationships, active learning, learning communities, and models specific to minority-serving institutions and community colleges.

Major Assignments

Movie Analysis — "Mind-Body Learning: Sites of Individual Learning in Larry Crowne (2011)"

Analyzed Tom Hanks's Larry Crowne through the lens of sites of learning and what learning does, arguing that Crowne's journey illustrates how learning occurs across geographic, cognitive, and embodied sites. The essay drew on Pratt's five teaching perspectives to examine how instructors in the film engage different modes of facilitation, and on Freiler's concept of embodied learning to argue that Crowne's participation in the moped club represents a form of knowledge that bypasses conscious language systems. Synthesized scholarship from Brookfield, Buckley and Lee, Rogers, and Säljö to frame learning as a continual process of experiencing, interpreting, and reflecting rather than a product or outcome.

Novel Analysis — "Individuality in Context: Sociocultural Factors, Individuality, and Constructed Learning Landscapes in Learning by Heart (2020)"

Examined Tony Wagner's memoir through two sociocultural lenses: the hidden curriculum and the multiplicity of space in learning environments. Argued that Wagner's early alienation from peers illustrates how the hidden curriculum's unwritten social rules disadvantage students who lack access to informal peer networks, drawing on Gair and Mullins's five categories of hidden curriculum. Applied Beyes and Michels's concept of performative space and Lefebvre's threefold ontology of physical, mental, and social space to analyze how Wagner's capacity for learning depended on his ability to perform identity within relational university spaces.

Topic Paper — "Ivy League Blues: Columbia University GS Student Resilience and Persistence"

Conducted a case study of the Equality for GS movement at Columbia University's School of General Studies, examining how institutional financial aid disparities between GS and Columbia College created barriers to persistence for non-traditional students. Analyzed the movement's origins in a student's senior thesis and connected institutional data to Magolda's research on the affective dimension of learning and faculty-student relationships as drivers of intellectual development and motivation.

Discussion Posts & Reflections

Contributed modular discussion posts engaging with course readings and peer responses across all three modules. In Module 1, explored how teaching functions as a negotiation between understanding an audience and creating environments with maximum potential for learning, drawing on Kolb's experiential learning cycle. In Module 2, reflected on personal experience with the Warrior-Scholar Project and the Mellon Mays Undergraduate Fellowship as examples of how extra-curricular programs address the hidden curriculum's gaps in career preparation and socialization for non-traditional students. In Module 3, argued that creating physical, social, and psychological space for students — supported by institutional investment in innovative teaching strategies — is central to improving student persistence.
