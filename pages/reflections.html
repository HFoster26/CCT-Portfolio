CCTI 2025 Reflections

Structured reflections from the Certification in College Teaching Institute, May 8–9, 2025, Michigan State University. Each session asked three questions: what I learned, what I’m still uncertain about, and how I’ll apply the material to my own teaching.

Understanding the University Context

Thursday, May 8, 2025 · Morning

What skills and techniques did I learn?

This session helped me understand how dramatically teaching expectations shift across institutional types. Reading the mission statements and promotion criteria side by side—MSU’s emphasis on research-driven instruction versus Calvin College’s framing of teaching as “the primary vocation” versus Jackson Community College’s focus on student access and career preparation—made concrete something I’d understood abstractly from Neumann, Parry, and Becher’s disciplinary framework in EAD 871. The practical takeaway is that my teaching philosophy needs to be adaptable: the formula (two parts humor, one part content, one part digital tools, one part metacognition) stays constant, but how I frame it in a job letter changes depending on whether I’m writing to an R1 that wants to see research-teaching integration or a liberal arts college that wants to see undergraduate mentorship at the center.

What am I still uncertain about?

I want to better understand how minority-serving institutions like Howard articulate their teaching expectations in practice, not just in the faculty handbook. As someone building a digital humanities project centered on Black cultural heritage, MSIs and HBCUs are institutions where my research and teaching would be deeply aligned with the institutional mission. I need to investigate how the intersection of cultural mission and teaching expectations operates at those institutions beyond what the mission statement says on paper.

How can I apply this?

The most direct application is in how I write learning objectives for my courses. The session made clear that institutions define student success differently—MSU emphasizes “globally engaged citizen leaders,” while community colleges emphasize career readiness and transfer preparation. When I design my IAH 207 and ENG 142 courses, I should be more explicit about connecting my learning goals (semiotic reading, identity analysis, metacognitive writing) to the institutional context my students are actually in. At MSU, that means framing humanities inquiry as preparation for engaged citizenship, not just disciplinary skill-building.

Creating Effective Learning Environments

Thursday, May 8, 2025 · Afternoon

What skills and techniques did I learn?

This session reinforced something I’ve been building toward intuitively but hadn’t fully theorized: that the learning environment is not a backdrop to teaching but a pedagogical instrument in itself. The physical space, the social dynamics, the psychological safety of the room—these are things I actively construct, not things that happen to exist. My day-one fear icebreaker in IAH 207 is exactly this kind of environment-building: by sharing vulnerability (kangaroos, personal success), I’m establishing the social contract of the room before any content is introduced. The session gave me a more precise vocabulary for what humor does in my classroom—it is an environment-building tool that creates the conditions for intellectual risk-taking.

What am I still uncertain about?

I’m uncertain about how to scale effective learning environments into larger class formats. My formula works well in sections of 25–30 students where I can read the room and adjust in real time. When I taught the 90-person Afrofuturism recitation, the humor had to land differently—bigger, faster, less conversational. I need to investigate more structured strategies for building psychological safety and intellectual risk-taking in large lecture environments where individual connection is harder to establish.

How can I apply this?

I can be more intentional about the first ten minutes of every class session, not just day one. The fear icebreaker sets the environment at the start of the semester, but effective learning environments need to be maintained and rebuilt throughout. I want to develop brief, recurring opening activities—tied to the course content—that re-establish the atmosphere of play and risk-taking before moving into analytical work. The Gothic horror group drawing activity is a model for this: it builds the environment and delivers content simultaneously. I want more activities that do that double duty across different units.

Assessing Student Learning

Friday, May 9, 2025 · Morning

What skills and techniques did I learn?

I learned about Bloom’s Taxonomy as a framework for writing measurable learning objectives across cognitive levels—Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create. The revised taxonomy (Krathwohl, 2001) reorganizes the original hierarchy and adds a knowledge dimension that distinguishes between factual, conceptual, procedural, and metacognitive knowledge.

What am I still uncertain about?

I think I need to investigate creating qualitative learning objectives more. Much of the assessment literature is oriented toward measurable, quantifiable outcomes, but in a soft pure discipline like English, the most important learning—the development of critical perspectives, the capacity for interpretation, the ability to sit with ambiguity—resists precise measurement. I want to find frameworks that honor the qualitative nature of humanities learning without abandoning rigor.

How can I apply this?

I can be more intentional about how I define success in the classroom by using frameworks that are more aligned with what I actually want students to do. Many of my most effective activities already operate at upper levels of Bloom’s—the AI literacy assignment at “Evaluate” (students appraising AI output against humanistic standards), the Gothic horror drawing activity at “Create” (students producing original semiotic artifacts)—but I haven’t been articulating those levels explicitly in my syllabi or assignment descriptions. Writing measurable objectives using Bloom’s vocabulary would make the pedagogical purpose of each activity visible to students and reviewers alike.

Incorporating Technology in Teaching

Friday, May 9, 2025 · Afternoon

What skills and techniques did I learn?

The Technology Jam exercise reinforced the principle I already follow in my teaching: never name a tool without explaining what learning it enables. The session’s framework—start with a learning objective, then choose the technology that serves it—is the right sequence, and it’s the sequence I used when I designed the AI literacy assignment for my Afrofuturism sections. I didn’t start with “I want to use ChatGPT in class.” I started with “I want students to articulate what humanistic inquiry contributes that computational pattern-matching cannot,” and then chose the three-platform comparison as the method that would get them there. The session also introduced me to the Technology Jam Figma board as a collaborative design tool, which is itself an example of technology serving a pedagogical purpose—it made the group design process visible and shareable in real time.

What am I still uncertain about?

I need to think more carefully about accessibility and technology. My Gothic horror activity uses Google Drawings, which works well for sighted students in a computer-equipped classroom, but I haven’t fully considered how that activity would work for a student using assistive technology or for a class meeting in a room without reliable internet. The general principle—technology as infrastructure, not decoration—needs to be paired with a commitment to ensuring that the infrastructure is accessible to every student in the room.

How can I apply this?

The most immediate application is using Bloom’s Taxonomy (from the assessment session) to write more precise learning objectives for my technology-integrated activities. For the AI literacy assignment, the current implicit objective operates at the “Evaluate” level of Bloom’s (students are appraising and judging AI output against humanistic standards), but I haven’t articulated that explicitly in my course materials. Writing measurable learning objectives using Bloom’s vocabulary would make the pedagogical purpose of each technology choice visible to students and to anyone reviewing my teaching portfolio. It would also help me design assessments that actually measure whether the technology-integrated activity achieved what I intended.
