Five Core Teaching Competencies

1. Discipline-Related Teaching Strategies

CCTI Session: Understanding the University Context

May 8, 2025 · CCTI, Michigan State University

Examined how institutional type shapes teaching expectations and how disciplinary identity (English as a “soft pure” discipline per Neumann, Parry, and Becher) determines appropriate pedagogical methods. Analyzed mission statements and promotion criteria across five institution types to understand how the same teaching practice is valued differently in different contexts.

Application: The Formula in Practice

My discipline-specific teaching strategy is organized around a ratio: two parts humor, one part content, one part digital tools, one part metacognition. In a soft pure discipline where the goal is developing critical perspectives rather than accumulating fixed knowledge, the pedagogical sequence matters: I move from play to analysis, using humor as both delivery mechanism and environment-builder. The Gothic horror group drawing activity and the AI literacy assignment are discipline-specific strategies designed for the interpretive, qualitative, discussion-based work that English pedagogy requires.

Coursework: EAD 871 — Collegiate Contexts of Teaching and Learning

Fall 2023 · Michigan State University

Studied disciplinary differences in teaching and learning, completed the Teaching Perspectives Inventory (dominant perspective: Nurturing; near-dominant: Developmental), and engaged with Neumann, Parry, and Becher’s framework for understanding how knowledge structures shape pedagogy across hard/soft, pure/applied disciplines.

2. Creating Inclusive Learning Environments

CCTI Session: Creating Effective Learning Environments

May 8, 2025 · CCTI, Michigan State University

Explored the physical, social, and psychological dimensions of learning environments. Examined how instructor behavior and classroom design create or constrain conditions for intellectual risk-taking. Connected session concepts to my existing practice of using humor as an environment-building tool.

Application: The Hidden Curriculum and Inclusive Structures

As a non-traditional first-generation college and graduate student, I am intimately familiar with the gaps that the hidden curriculum creates (Gair and Mullins). My own trajectory—from the Marine Corps through the Warrior-Scholar Project and the Mellon Mays Undergraduate Fellowship to MSU—taught me that inclusive teaching is both a disposition and a set of deliberate practices that make visible what the institution leaves implicit. The day-one fear icebreaker in IAH 207 is a concrete example: by modeling vulnerability and establishing a social contract of mutual risk-taking before any content is introduced, I build an environment where veterans, first-generation students, and students from all backgrounds can engage on equal footing.

3. Assessment of Student Learning

CCTI Session: Assessing Student Learning

May 9, 2025 · CCTI, Michigan State University

Introduced to Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (Krathwohl, 2001) as a framework for writing measurable learning objectives. Discussed alignment between learning objectives, instructional activities, and assessment methods across cognitive levels from Remember through Create.

Application: Formative Assessment as Dialogue

My writing center background trained me to treat feedback as dialogue rather than verdict. Revision is discovery, not correction. When I return student writing, I ask questions that push students to examine their own reasoning—not “fix this paragraph” but “what assumption is doing the heavy lifting in this argument, and what happens to your thesis if that assumption is wrong?” My most effective activities already operate at upper Bloom’s levels: the AI literacy assignment at Evaluate, the Gothic horror activity at Create. The CCTI session pushed me to articulate those levels explicitly in my syllabi and assignment descriptions.

4. Technology Incorporation

CCTI Session: Incorporating Technology in Teaching

May 9, 2025 · CCTI, Michigan State University

Participated in a Technology Jam exercise designing a technology-integrated learning activity for a 75-student gen-ed course. Practiced the sequence of starting from a learning objective and selecting technology that serves it, rather than choosing a tool and building an activity around it.

Application: AI Literacy Assignment — IAH 207

Designed and implemented an AI literacy assignment in which three student groups each used a different generative AI platform (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini) to produce outlines on Afrofuturism and Black speculative practice, then compared outputs to identify what humanistic inquiry contributes that computational pattern-matching cannot replicate. The assignment follows the principle reinforced at CCTI: never name a tool without explaining what learning it enables.

CHI Fellowship: Digital Humanities as Pedagogy

2025–2026 · Cultural Heritage Informatics Fellowship, MSU

Building an interactive digital humanities archive using Leaflet.js (geospatial visualization) and D3.js (network analysis) as part of the Cultural Heritage Informatics Fellowship. The project demonstrates how digital tools can serve as scholarly arguments—geographic data as cultural evidence, network graphs as relational analysis—a practice that directly informs how I teach students to think about the relationship between technology and interpretation.

5. Understanding the University Context

CCTI Session: Aligning Teaching with Institutional Types & Missions

May 8, 2025 · CCTI, Michigan State University

Analyzed mission statements and teaching expectations across research universities (MSU), comprehensive universities (Grand Valley State), liberal arts colleges (Calvin College), minority-serving institutions (Howard University), and community colleges (Jackson Community College). Examined how promotion and tenure criteria reflect institutional values and how the same teaching practice is evaluated differently depending on context.

Application: Adaptive Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy is structured as a portable formula that adapts to institutional context. At an R1 like MSU, I frame the formula through research-teaching integration: the same analytical methods I use in my dissertation research are the methods I teach students to practice. At a liberal arts college, the emphasis shifts to undergraduate mentorship and the development of transferable critical thinking skills. At an MSI or HBCU, the cultural mission alignment becomes central—my work on Black cultural heritage positions my teaching as an extension of the institution’s core purpose. The formula stays constant; the framing is adaptive.
