Professional Development Activities

Throughout the CCT program, I have participated in workshops and seminars to deepen my understanding of teaching and learning in higher education.

Certification in College Teaching Institute (CCTI)

May 8–9, 2025 · Michigan State University · Graduate School / GREAT Office

A two-day intensive institute covering the five core competency areas required for the Certificate in College Teaching. The institute included sessions on institutional context, learning environments, assessment, and technology integration, with structured reflection activities throughout.

Understanding the University Context

Thursday, May 8, 2025 · Morning Session

Examined how institutional mission shapes teaching expectations across institution types: research universities (MSU), comprehensive universities (Grand Valley State), liberal arts colleges (Calvin College), minority-serving institutions (Howard University), and community colleges (Jackson Community College). Analyzed mission statements and promotion/tenure criteria side by side to understand how the same teaching practice is valued differently depending on institutional context.

Key Takeaway: Teaching philosophy must be adaptable to institutional mission. The formula stays constant; the framing shifts depending on whether the institution prioritizes research-teaching integration (R1), undergraduate mentorship (liberal arts), cultural mission (MSI), or student access (community college).

CCT Competency: University Context

Creating Effective Learning Environments

Thursday, May 8, 2025 · Afternoon Session

Explored the physical, social, and psychological dimensions of learning environments. Examined how classroom design, instructor behavior, and social dynamics create or constrain conditions for intellectual risk-taking. Discussed strategies for building inclusive learning spaces that support diverse student populations.

Key Takeaway: The learning environment is a pedagogical instrument, not a backdrop. Environment-building activities like the day-one fear icebreaker in IAH 207 are not warm-ups—they are deliberate constructions of the social contract that governs the rest of the semester.

CCT Competency: Inclusive Learning Environments

Assessing Student Learning

Friday, May 9, 2025 · Morning Session

Introduced Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (Krathwohl, 2001) as a framework for writing measurable learning objectives across cognitive levels: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create. Discussed alignment between learning objectives, instructional activities, and assessment methods.

Key Takeaway: Many of my most effective activities already operate at upper levels of Bloom’s (the AI literacy assignment at “Evaluate,” the Gothic horror activity at “Create”), but I haven’t been articulating those levels explicitly in my course materials. Writing measurable objectives using Bloom’s vocabulary makes the pedagogical purpose visible.

CCT Competency: Assessment

Incorporating Technology in Teaching

Friday, May 9, 2025 · Afternoon Session

Participated in a Technology Jam exercise: given a learning context (75-student introductory gen-ed course), groups designed a technology-integrated learning activity starting from a learning objective, then selecting the appropriate tool. Used a collaborative Figma board for group design work. Discussed alignment between technology choices and pedagogical goals.

Key Takeaway: Never name a tool without explaining what learning it enables. The correct sequence is objective first, technology second—exactly the design logic behind the AI literacy assignment in IAH 207, where the three-platform comparison was chosen because it served the epistemological goal, not because AI is trendy.
